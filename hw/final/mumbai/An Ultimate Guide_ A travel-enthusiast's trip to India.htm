Planning a trip to India?

I have lived in Bangalore, India for 10 years & have visited most of the major cities. Here is a guide from a native who moved to India as an expat.

Must-See Cities

New Delhi (दिल्ली)

As the capital of India, and home to one of the seven wonders of the modern world, the Taj Mahal, New Delhi is a must when visiting India. It is in the center of the country, and a huge airline hub so, a convenient first stop on your trip. Here you can visit the India Gate, the Qutab Minar, Red Fort, Jama Masjid and several other iconic historical sights. You can walk around and eat authentic cuisine like Mutton Seekh Kabab at Chandni Chowk, the main street of old Delhi, a shocking contrast to the wide, orderly streets of New Delhi. Cars, cycle rickshaws, hand-pulled carts, pedestrians, and animals all compete for space.

Mumbai (मुम्बई)

Mumbai is the most socially and economically developed city in India. It is home to all the Bollywood celebrities, and some of the richest people in the world. However, this is the cause to the large income gap between the rich and the poor. As, it is also known for the largest slums in the world. Personally, visiting a city like Mumbai not only is a culturally eye-opening experience, but also provides a tourist with perspective. Mumbai is much like a hustling bustling city in America. You must sit by Marina Drive eating a samosa, visit the elephanta caves, eat the world famous Pav Bhaji and Goli Pav, and also explore the thriving nightlife if you are 21+!

Exploring the Attractions

Going Outside the Major Cities: Amritsar

Chinese cities are extrordinary, but they should not be your only travel destinations in China. To fully immerse yourself in and experience the cultural differences in China, I strongly recommend visiting the Gansu Province. Located in Northwest China, Gansu has a unique cradle of Chinese culture. Over history and primarily attributed to the Silk Road, Han Chinese, Mongols, Turks and Tibetans all reside and co-exist the Gansu Province. The landmarks below are just a few of the treasures located in this part of China.

Labrang Monastery

The Labrang Monastery is one of the largest Tibetan Monasteries in China. Experience buddhist structures, yak butter sculptures, and the longest line of prayer wheels in the world.

Fun Fact: The famous Chinese movie "World Without Thieves (2004)" was filmed here.

The Rainbow Mountains of China

One of National Geographic's 'Top 10 Geographical Wonders of the World', The Rainbow Mountains of China were formed after billions of years of the climate's effect on the land. This treasure is tucked away in its remote area and is a trip in it of its self. It's like standing in a sea of fire, but you'd have to see it for yourself. Traveler's tip: the colors are most vibrant when it's raining.

Mogao Caves

Built in the year 366, and that is not a typo, the Mogao Caves were started by a monk who saw the vision of 1,000 Budhas. The construction lasted 10 dynasties and these caves are still standing to this day. In one of the caves (middle picture) is the second largest Budha sculpture ever! The caves are dark and they may or may not let you use your phone flashlight because technically no photos are allowed. Whoops!

Mingsha Mountain and Crescent Lake

Insterested in riding cammels and going sledding down sand dunes? Visit Mingsha Mountain. Also called the Echoing Sand Mountain, these dunes are known for the humming noise they make when the wind blows. Don't forget suncreen! The sun is unforgivable. You will also find piles of sand wherever to tred for the next few days. Worth it!

Travel Tips

Packing Tips

**Please note: While shorts may be comfortable and preferable in some places due to warm temperatures, only wear shorts that are mid-thigh length or longer. It is culturally respectful. Except in major cities where western influence is more apparent, shorts are rarely worn in China. “Short shorts” and spaghetti straps are inappropriate.

Ziploc Bags: These are perfect for storing small items and items that could leak.

Baggage: Review the Airline Baggage Rules and TSA Guidelines. Most checked luggage cannot be over 50lbs.

Laundry: Most hotels have laundry service. Laundry dropped off in the morning is finished by the evening. If you plan on doing this, make sure to budget extra spending money for the service. In addition, you could pack Woolite or a small container of detergent and do your own laundry.

Do I need a Visa?

Yes. You need a travel Visa. Apply at least 3 months in advance.

Electronics and Converters

Chinese hotels have hairdryers. No converters are necessary due to the fact that modern electronics auto-matically make the conversion. No adapters are needed

Will My Cell Phone Work in China?

Yes. Call your cell service provider to let them know that you will be traveling in China and find out about the best in-ternational plan for your needs. Be careful about using cell data in China. Use the internet when you have wifi access.

Do I Need Vaccinations to go to China?

Vaccinations are NOT REQUIRED for China. The Hepatitis A (food-borne illness) vaccination is recommended. Please speak with your doctor for a list of other recommended vaccinations, many of which you may already have had.

Probiotics

Consider taking a probiotic, active yeasts and live bacteria that are good for your digestive system, for a week or two before departure and for the duration of the trip.

Can I Drink the Water in China?

No. Even the locals do not drink the water. Most hotels have two complimentary bottles of water per day. It is fine to use the water to brush your teeth, wash up and shower. If you are concerned, however, you could boil the water in the room for tooth brushing. There is a kettle provided in every hotel room.

Culture Shock

  • Some beds are HARD. We’re not talking “firm”, we’re talking “plywood-like”.
  • Locals will stare at you, take pictures of you and ask you to take pictures with them. Enjoy your 5 minutes of fame in China!
  • Squat toilets. Enough said. Bring some pocket packets of tissues

The Language

In both Shanghai and Beijing, Mandarin is the most common language. Below are very useful words and phrases to use while comminicating with the Chinese.

Greetings

Hello

你好 (Nǐ hǎo)

My Name Is

我的叫“name” (Wǒ de jiào "name")

Thank You

谢谢 (Xièxiè)

You're Welcome

不客气 (Bù kèqì)

Goodbye

再见 (Zàijiàn)

Shopping

How Much?

多少? (Duōshǎo?)

Too Expensive

太贵了 (Tài guìle)

Make it a little cheaper

便宜一点 (Piányí yīdiǎn)

General and Helpful

Where is the bathroom

洗手间在哪里 (Xǐshǒujiān zài nǎlǐ)

I Don't Understand

我不懂 (Wǒ bù dǒng)
